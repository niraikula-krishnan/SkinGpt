DISEASE: Vitiligo

OVERVIEW:
Vitiligo is a chronic autoimmune condition where the immune system destroys melanocytes (pigment-producing cells), causing white patches to appear on skin. It can affect any area of the body and is more visible on darker skin tones.

CAUSES:
- Autoimmune destruction of melanocytes
- Genetic predisposition — family history increases risk
- Triggers: sunburn, skin trauma, stress, chemical exposure
- Associated with other autoimmune diseases: thyroid disorders, type 1 diabetes, alopecia areata

SYMPTOMS:
- White or depigmented patches on skin
- Patches most common on face, hands, arms, feet, genitals
- Hair in affected areas may turn white
- Patches may expand over time
- Premature whitening of hair, eyelashes, eyebrows
- No pain or itching in most cases

TYPES:
- Generalized: most common, widespread patches on many body parts
- Segmental: affects only one side or segment of body, more stable
- Focal: limited to one or few areas
- Universal: rare, affects most of the body surface

TREATMENT:
- Topical corticosteroids to restore pigment in early stages
- Topical calcineurin inhibitors: tacrolimus, pimecrolimus
- Ruxolitinib cream (Opzelura): FDA-approved targeted therapy
- Phototherapy: narrowband UVB light therapy — most effective treatment
- Oral JAK inhibitors for widespread vitiligo
- Skin grafting or melanocyte transplantation for stable vitiligo
- Cosmetic camouflage makeup and self-tanner

IS IT CONTAGIOUS: No, vitiligo is not contagious.

WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR:
- White patches appearing or spreading on skin
- Family history of vitiligo or autoimmune diseases
- Patches on face or other visible areas causing distress
- Symptoms of associated thyroid disease: fatigue, weight changes

PREVENTION:
- No known way to prevent vitiligo
- Protect depigmented patches with SPF 50+ sunscreen as they burn easily
- Manage stress
- Regular thyroid function tests if diagnosed
