DISEASE: Seborrheic Keratoses
ALSO KNOWN AS: Seborrhoeic Warts, Senile Warts, Barnacles

OVERVIEW:
Seborrheic keratoses are common noncancerous skin growths that appear as waxy, wart-like brown or black raised spots. They become more common with age and are completely harmless.

CAUSES:
- Exact cause unknown
- Age — become more common after age 50
- Genetics and family history
- Sun exposure may contribute
- Not caused by infection or virus

SYMPTOMS:
- Round or oval shaped growths
- Waxy, scaly, slightly raised surface
- Color: light tan to brown to black
- Appear stuck on to the skin like a barnacle
- Range from tiny to over 1 inch
- Can appear anywhere except palms and soles
- Usually appear in large numbers

TREATMENT:
- No treatment required as they are benign
- Removal for cosmetic reasons or if irritated
- Cryotherapy: liquid nitrogen
- Curettage: scraping off
- Electrosurgery: burning off with electric current
- Laser removal

IS IT CONTAGIOUS: No.

WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR:
- Sudden appearance of many seborrheic keratoses may rarely signal internal cancer
- Lesion that bleeds, is painful, or looks unusual
- Uncertain if growth is seborrheic keratosis or melanoma

PREVENTION:
- No proven prevention
- Sunscreen may slow development
