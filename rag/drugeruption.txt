DISEASE: Drug Eruption
ALSO KNOWN AS: Drug Rash, Medication Reaction, Adverse Drug Reaction

OVERVIEW:
Drug eruption is a skin reaction caused by a medication. It is one of the most common adverse drug reactions and can range from mild rashes to life-threatening conditions like Stevens-Johnson syndrome.

CAUSES:
- Allergic immune response to a medication
- Common culprits: antibiotics (penicillin, sulfa), NSAIDs, anticonvulsants, allopurinol
- Can occur days to weeks after starting a new medication
- Sometimes caused by drug interactions

SYMPTOMS:
- Morbilliform (measles-like) rash: most common type, red flat or raised spots
- Urticaria (hives): itchy raised welts
- Photosensitivity: rash on sun-exposed areas
- Fixed drug eruption: same spot reacts every time drug is taken
- Stevens-Johnson syndrome (severe): painful blistering of skin and mucous membranes
- Itching, burning sensation

TREATMENT:
- Stop the offending medication immediately (under medical supervision)
- Antihistamines for itching
- Topical or oral corticosteroids for severe reactions
- Emergency care for Stevens-Johnson syndrome

IS IT CONTAGIOUS: No.

WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR:
- Rash appears after starting a new medication
- Blistering, swelling of face or throat, difficulty breathing — seek emergency care
- Rash spreading rapidly

PREVENTION:
- Inform doctors of all drug allergies
- Carry a medical alert card if allergic to common medications
- Never restart a medication that caused a previous reaction without medical advice
