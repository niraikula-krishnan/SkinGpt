DISEASE: Moles
ALSO KNOWN AS: Nevi (singular: Nevus), Melanocytic Nevi

OVERVIEW:
Moles are common skin growths that appear as small, dark brown spots caused by clusters of pigmented cells called melanocytes. Most people have between 10 and 40 moles. Most moles are harmless but some can develop into melanoma, a serious form of skin cancer.

CAUSES:
- Clusters of melanocytes (pigment-producing cells) growing together
- Sun exposure increases the number and darkness of moles
- Genetics and family history
- Hormonal changes during puberty and pregnancy can make moles darker or larger

SYMPTOMS:
- Round or oval shaped spots
- Flat or slightly raised
- Smooth or wrinkled surface
- Color ranges from pink, tan, brown, to dark brown or black
- Usually less than 6mm in diameter
- Can appear anywhere on the body including scalp, between toes, under nails

TYPES:
- Common moles: small, single-color, well-defined borders
- Congenital nevi: moles present at birth, higher risk of melanoma
- Dysplastic nevi (atypical moles): larger, irregular borders, mixed colors, higher risk of becoming cancerous
- Spitz nevus: dome-shaped, pink or multicolored, common in children

ABCDE WARNING SIGNS (potential melanoma):
- A - Asymmetry: one half does not match the other
- B - Border: edges are irregular, ragged, or blurred
- C - Color: multiple shades of brown, black, red, white, or blue
- D - Diameter: larger than 6mm (size of a pencil eraser)
- E - Evolving: any change in size, shape, color, or new symptom like bleeding

TREATMENT:
- Most moles do not need treatment
- Surgical excision if mole shows atypical features or for cosmetic reasons
- Shave excision for raised moles
- Laser removal for flat cosmetic moles
- Never attempt to remove a mole at home

IS IT CONTAGIOUS: No, moles are not contagious.

COMPLICATIONS:
- Melanoma — a life-threatening skin cancer that can develop from atypical moles
- Irritation from clothing or jewelry rubbing on moles

WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR:
- Any mole that changes in size, shape, or color
- A mole that bleeds, itches, or becomes painful
- New moles appearing after age 30
- Any mole that matches ABCDE warning criteria
- Moles that look different from others on your body (ugly duckling sign)

PREVENTION:
- Apply broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher sunscreen daily
- Avoid sun exposure between 10am and 4pm
- Wear protective clothing and hats
- Avoid tanning beds
- Perform monthly self-skin examinations
- Annual dermatology check for people with many or atypical moles
